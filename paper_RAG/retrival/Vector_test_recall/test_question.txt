Part 1: Research Background and Abstract
1. Q: What is the core research theme of this paper?
A: The paper focuses on Deep Neural Network (DNN) inference in Vehicular Edge Computing (VEC), specifically researching the problem of load balancing through joint model deployment and task offloading.

2. Q: What is the optimization goal of this research?
A: The objective is to minimize the maximum workload of the Roadside Units (RSUs) within the VEC system.

3. Q: What constraints are considered in the optimization problem?
A: The problem considers constraints such as the maximum tolerable response time for each inference task and the specific inference accuracy requirements.

4. Q: What technology do intelligent vehicle applications (e.g., autonomous driving, pedestrian detection) primarily rely on?
A: These applications rely on trained Deep Neural Network (DNN) models to perform inference on real-time input data.

5. Q: Why are modern vehicles often unable to complete all inference requests locally?
A: Although the computational power of modern vehicles has improved significantly, DNN inference is highly resource-intensive. The time required for local processing far exceeds the real-time requirements of the inference tasks.

6. Q: What is Model Deployment?
A: Model deployment refers to the process of installing trained DNN models on RSUs to provide services for corresponding inference tasks.

7. Q: What is Task Offloading?
A: Task offloading refers to scheduling an inference task from one RSU to another that possesses more computational resources to accelerate execution and improve Quality of Service (QoS).

8. Q: Why is load balancing for DNN inference important in VEC?
A: Since RSUs have limited computational resources, a lack of effective load balancing during simultaneous high-volume task arrivals leads to some RSUs being overloaded while others remain idle. This increases response latency and degrades overall system performance.

9. Q: What algorithm does the paper propose to solve the joint optimization of model deployment and task offloading?
A: The paper proposes a two-stage optimization algorithm called the LAMA-GAP algorithm.

10. Q: What are the responsibilities of the two stages of the algorithm?
A: The first stage develops a load-aware matching strategy (for model deployment and task offloading), while the second stage designs a strategy to offload tasks between the CPU and GPU within an RSU to further achieve load balancing.

Part 2: System Model and Resource Constraints
11. Q: What are the main components of the system architecture constructed in the paper?
A: The system architecture consists of multiple vehicles, multiple RSUs, and a central cloud.

12. Q: How do vehicles submit their inference tasks?
A: Vehicles offload inference tasks to the RSUs within their communication range via Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) communication.

13. Q: How do RSUs communicate with each other?
A: RSUs are connected via wired links, which allows inference tasks to be offloaded from one RSU to another.

14. Q: Are all RSUs equipped with GPUs?
A: No. All RSUs are equipped with CPUs, but due to resource and cost considerations, only a portion of the RSUs are equipped with GPUs.

15. Q: What role does the cloud play in the architecture?
A: The cloud stores all trained inference models, which RSUs can download via wired connections.

16. Q: How do RSUs process the task queue?
A: RSUs process tasks in the task queue using a First-In-First-Out (FIFO) approach.

17. Q: How many accuracy levels does the paper consider for each DNN model?
A: Three different levels of inference accuracy are considered: low, medium, and high.

18. Q: What is the tradeoff between model accuracy and response latency?
A: Achieving lower inference latency may come at the cost of inference accuracy; conversely, higher-precision models typically require more storage space and longer inference times.

19. Q: Given limited RSU storage, what hypothesis does the paper propose regarding model deployment?
A: The paper assumes that for each type of model, an RSU can deploy at most one accuracy level.

20. Q: What specific information is contained in an inference request packet (tuple)?
A: It includes the input data size, requested DNN model type, minimum required accuracy level, the RSU that initially submitted the request, and the maximum tolerable response latency.

21. Q: What is the model loading process when a task is executed on a GPU?
A: Because the GPU cannot directly access the CPU main memory, the corresponding model must first be initialized in the CPU memory and then loaded into the GPU's Video RAM (VRAM).

22. Q: What two parts comprise the total storage space occupied by an RSU?
A: It includes the storage space occupied by the deployed DNN models and the storage space occupied by the input data.

23. Q: Under what conditions is transmission delay generated?
A: Transmission delay is only generated when an inference task is offloaded to an RSU different from the one to which it was initially submitted.

24. Q: How is the workload (Load) of an RSU calculated?
A: The workload of an RSU is equal to the total latency required to complete all inference tasks scheduled to that RSU.

25. Q: Does the system model allow vehicles to process inference tasks locally?
A: No. Due to limited computational resources, vehicles are not permitted to process inference tasks locally.

Part 3: Problem Formulation and Algorithm Design
26. Q: What does constraint (14) in the optimization problem ensure?
A: It ensures that each inference task is offloaded to exactly one RSU for processing.

27. Q: What is the function of constraint (16)?
A: It ensures that an inference task is only routed to an RSU that already hosts its corresponding DNN model.

28. Q: What is the constraint regarding GPU usage?
A: Constraint (17) ensures that an inference task is only assigned to a GPU if the target RSU is equipped with one.

29. Q: What does the acronym LAMA stand for?
A: Load-Aware Matching Algorithm.

30. Q: What is the core idea of a Matching Game?
A: A matching game is a mathematical framework used to model the matching process between two sets of entities, each with its own preferences; it is widely used in resource allocation and network optimization.

31. Q: What does an inference task prioritize when establishing its preference list in LAMA?
A: Tasks prefer RSUs that can minimize their response latency to ensure the task is completed before the deadline.

32. Q: What does an RSU prioritize when establishing its preference list in LAMA?
A: RSUs prefer to execute tasks that result in the smallest increment to their workload to prevent high loads from affecting subsequent tasks.

33. Q: How does LAMA sort tasks before starting the matching process?
A: LAMA first sorts tasks in ascending order based on their maximum tolerable latency.

34. Q: How does the algorithm handle an RSU that is in a state of maximum load during LAMA matching?
A: If an RSU has the highest load and not all unmatched tasks prefer it most, the algorithm skips it and continues matching with other RSUs.

35. Q: What does the acronym GAP stand for?
A: GPU-Accelerated Task Processing Algorithm.

36. Q: What is the primary purpose of the GAP algorithm?
A: GAP determines the scheduling policy from tasks to devices (CPU or GPU) to identify which tasks should be executed on the GPU, while further ensuring system-wide load balancing.

37. Q: In the GAP algorithm, what action is taken if calculations show that using a GPU improves performance?
A: If the GPU improves performance, it will be used to execute all tasks assigned to that RSU; otherwise, the system rolls back to the initial saved state.

38. Q: What problem is caused by the uneven distribution of tasks between busy urban areas and suburbs?
A: It leads to uneven load distribution, where RSUs in busy areas may become overloaded while RSU resources in suburbs remain idle.

39. Q: What is the biggest difference between LAMA and traditional matching algorithms?
A: LAMA incorporates system load as a core factor, ensuring that the matching of tasks to RSUs promotes overall system load balancing.

40. Q: What information does the first step of the GAP algorithm need to identify?
A: It identifies the dominant task type and its highest accuracy requirement on the RSUs currently equipped with GPUs.

Part 4: Experimental Simulation and Result Comparison
41. Q: Which three specific DNN inference tasks were tested in the experimental simulation?
A: Object detection, image classification, and semantic segmentation.

42. Q: Specifically for the object detection task, which models with different accuracies were used?
A: YOLOv8s, YOLOv8m, and YOLOv8l models were used.

43. Q: What was the default maximum tolerable latency for all inference tasks in the simulation?
A: The default value was set to 4 seconds.

44. Q: Against which two baseline algorithms was LAMA-GAP compared?
A: It was compared against a Matching-based baseline (BA-MA) and a Greedy-strategy-based baseline (BA-GR).

45. Q: What is the device scheduling preference of the BA-MA baseline algorithm?
A: It prioritizes the CPU for task execution, as it assumes the GPU is only effective when processing a sufficient number of tasks of the same type.

46. Q: According to experimental results, by what percentage did LAMA-GAP reduce the maximum load compared to BA-MA when the number of tasks was 50?
A: It reduced the maximum load by 39.3%.

47. Q: What trend does the maximum load of all algorithms show as the number of RSUs increases? Why?
A: The maximum load shows a decreasing trend because more RSUs provide more available computational resources, giving tasks more options and thus lowering the maximum load.

48. Q: When the number of GPU-equipped RSUs increased to 5, how much did LAMA-GAP reduce the maximum load compared to BA-GR?
A: The maximum load was reduced by 42.9%.

49. Q: Why does increasing the number of pre-deployed DNN models on RSUs reduce the maximum load?
A: Because the probability that an RSU already possesses the required model increases significantly, which greatly reduces model deployment latency and thus lowers the RSU load.

50. Q: When the maximum tolerable latency was significantly reduced to 2 seconds, by how much did LAMA-GAP reduce the number of unfinished tasks compared to BA-MA?
A: The number of unfinished tasks was reduced by 84.9%.

Research Dataset: Two-Level Scheduling Algorithms for DNN Inference (Q51-100)

51. Q: What is the primary objective of the proposed two-level scheduling problem?
A: The objective is to minimize the total weighted sum of response time and energy consumption (total trade-off cost) for all DNN inference tasks.

52. Q: Which two levels of scheduling are jointly investigated in this paper?
A: The paper investigates task scheduling at the microarchitecture-level and the network-level.

53. Q: What are the three main constraints considered in the problem formulation?
A: The constraints include per-task response time, per-vehicle energy consumption, and per-vehicle storage capacity.

54. Q: Why does the paper focus on scheduling among vehicles instead of using Roadside Units (RSUs)?
A: Communication between vehicles and RSUs often suffers from high channel fading due to obstacles like buildings and larger distances compared to V2V connections.

55. Q: What is the computational complexity of the formulated two-level scheduling problem?
A: The paper proves that the formulated problem is NP-hard, specifically NP-complete.

56. Q: What does the acronym GTA stand for in this research?
A: GTA stands for Group Transformation based Algorithm.

57. Q: How does the GTA algorithm make scheduling decisions at the two different levels?
A: It makes network-level decisions using a group transformation approach and microarchitecture-level decisions using a greedy strategy.

58. Q: What is the "total trade-off cost" defined in the paper?
A: It is the total weighted sum of response time and energy consumption for all inference tasks in the vehicular network.

59. Q: Which deep reinforcement learning (DRL) algorithm is introduced for the proposed DRL-based scheduling?
A: Deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) is introduced.

60. Q: What is the purpose of the "Binding" stage in the DRL algorithm?
A: It binds layers with low and high computation delays together into blocks to reduce the exploratory action space and accelerate convergence.

61. Q: Which hardware platform is used to simulate the DNN accelerator?
A: Eyeriss is used as the representative DNN accelerator.

62. Q: What are the two universal computing units considered in the vehicle model?
A: The Central Processing Unit (CPU) and Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) are regarded as universal computing units.

63. Q: How is the response time of a task defined in this system?
A: It is the period from when a task arrives to when it is completed, comprising computation delay, communication delay, and waiting time.

64. Q: What is the role of the RSU in the described system model?
A: The RSU in the road segment is regarded as a local controller that obtains information from each vehicle within its communication coverage.

65. Q: Which protocol is used for Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) communication in the simulations?
A: V2V communication is implemented by protocol 802.11p in the NS-3 simulator.

66. Q: What are the two critical factors used to evaluate the Quality of Computing Services (QoS)?
A: Response time and energy consumption are the two critical factors.

67. Q: According to the paper, which layers can be processed by the Eyeriss accelerator?
A: The accelerator Eyeriss is customized for and only can accelerate convolution layers or fully connected layers.

68. Q: What does the "GTA_Net" strategy represent in the performance analysis?
A: It represents a strategy where GTA only schedules inference tasks at the network-level, with each vehicle processing tasks on its CPU.

69. Q: How is the "reward" defined in the pre-training stage (PRE) of the DRL algorithm?
A: It is defined as the difference between the total trade-off costs generated before and after executing an action for all layers of tasks in L.

70. Q: What happens to the reward if a scheduling decision violates the response time or energy constraints?
A: The reward is set to negative infinity (-inf) if constraints are not met.

71. Q: Which four DNN models were selected to evaluate the algorithms for heterogeneous tasks?
A: NiN, MobileNetV3-Small, ResNet18, and VGG16.

72. Q: In the system model, what is the default "effective capacitance" value used for energy calculation?
A: It is set to 10^-27 by default.

73. Q: What is the purpose of the "Memory Pool" in the DRL framework?
A: It is used to store scheduling experiences to facilitate random sampling for training, which improves stability.

74. Q: What is a "Task Group" according to Definition 2?
A: The set of the tasks located in a vehicle is called a task group for that vehicle.

75. Q: How does the paper prove Theorem 1 regarding NP-completeness?
A: It shows that a special case of the proposed problem P can be reduced to a 0-1 knapsack problem (KP).

76. Q: What is the specific data size of the input image for the DNN inference tasks?
A: The input is an image with 224x224x3 pixels and its data size is 147 KB.

77. Q: What is the "valuation" of a group family in the GTA algorithm?
A: The valuation of a group family is the sum of the valuations of all task groups it contains.

78. Q: How much did GTA and DRL reduce the trade-off cost on average compared to baseline algorithms?
A: GTA and DRL reduced the total weighted sum by 41.49% and 62.38%, respectively.

79. Q: Which simulator was used to generate vehicle trajectories?
A: Vehicle trajectories are generated by SUMO using the "randomTrips.py" script.

80. Q: What is the "GS" strategy mentioned in the algorithm section?
A: GS is a greedy strategy proposed to schedule an inference task at the microarchitecture-level.

81. Q: Why is the mobility of vehicles considered a major challenge for scheduling?
A: High mobility leads to rapid changes in relative locations and transmission rates, making offloading decisions based on current state potentially unsuitable for the next state.

82. Q: What are the weights alphai and betai used for in the objective function?
A: They reflect the preference and importance of response time and energy consumption, respectively, on the QoS of a task.

83. Q: What does the term "failed task" mean in the simulation context?
A: A failed task is one unsuccessfully executed due to data loss in communication or failure to satisfy problem constraints.

84. Q: Which CPU hardware was Intel-based in the microarchitecture-level simulation?
A: A desktop with an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-6700 CPU @ 3.4GHz.

85. Q: How often are the parameters of the target network updated in the DRL algorithm?
A: They are updated to match the parameters of the value network every v steps.

86. Q: What is the "Group Transformation Rule" (Definition 6)?
A: A group family G is transformed to G' if and only if the valuation of G' is higher than G.

87. Q: In the DRL state definition, what information does lij represent?
A: It represents the trade-off cost for layer rij(t) in the set of fully connected or convolution layers.

88. Q: What is the maximum speed limit set for vehicles in the SUMO simulation?
A: The maximum speed is set to 40 km/h, reflecting the real road environment limits.

89. Q: Which baseline algorithm (BA-TN) is inspired by coalition game theory?
A: BA-TN is customized based on coalition game theory for indivisible task scheduling at the network-level.

A: The contributions at the network-level are larger than those at the microarchitecture-level for both GTA and DRL.

91. Q: What map area was used for the road topology simulation?
A: A real road area of streets in Guangzhou Higher Education Mega Center obtained by OSM.

92. Q: What is the default number of hidden neurons used in the DRL and PRE models?
A: Both the value network and target network have one hidden layer with 128 neurons.

A: Constraint (41) ensures a later layer must be executed after the preceding layer is completed and its required input data is transferred.

94. Q: What does the indicator uk represent?
